Sustainability Development Goal 3: Tuberculosis

What are the Sustainability Goals?

The Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), established by the United Nations in 2015, are a universal call to action to end poverty, protect the planet, and ensure prosperity for all by 2030. There are 17 interconnected goals that address a wide range of global challenges, including poverty, inequality, climate change, environmental degradation, peace, and justice. We will cover SDG 3: Combating communicable diseases, focusing on the disease Tuberculosis.

Tuberculosis (TB) is a contagious bacterial infection primarily caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis. It predominantly affects the lungs but can also impact other organs. TB is a major global health concern due to its contagious nature and the risk it poses to individuals with weakened immune systems.

TB Fatality

The fatality rate of TB varies depending on several factors, including access to healthcare, the strain of the bacteria, and the patient's overall health. In general, TB can be fatal if left untreated, with an estimated global fatality rate of about 15%, making it one of the top infectious disease killers worldwide. However, early detection and appropriate treatment with antibiotics can significantly reduce the fatality rate and help individuals recover from TB. Public health efforts continue to focus on TB prevention, diagnosis, and treatment to further reduce its impact on global health.

Risk factors include close contact with individuals with active TB, weakened immune systems due to conditions like HIV/AIDS, malnutrition, and chronic illnesses, as well as behavioral factors such as substance abuse and smoking. Age, homelessness, incarceration, tra vel to high-incidence areas, and previous incomplete TB treatment can also elevate the risk. Additionally, healthcare workers and individuals in healthcare settings with inadequate infection control may face increased exposure risk. TB is a treatable disease, but awareness of these risk factors is crucial for prevention and early treatment.
